# The Discovery of DNA's Structure

The race to determine the structure of DNA is one of science's greatest detective stories — a tale of brilliant minds, overlooked contributions, and a discovery that launched the age of molecular biology.

![DNA structure diagram](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/DNA_Structure%2BKey%2BLabelled.pn_NoBB.png/400px-DNA_Structure%2BKey%2BLabelled.pn_NoBB.png)

## Background

- By the 1940s, scientists knew DNA carried genetic information
	- **1944 — Oswald Avery** showed DNA (not protein) was the "transforming principle"
	- **1952 — Hershey-Chase experiment** confirmed DNA is the genetic material
- The question was: **what does DNA look like**, and how does its structure enable heredity?

## The Key Players

### Rosalind Franklin (1920–1958)
- X-ray crystallographer at King's College London
- Produced **Photo 51** (1952) — the crucial X-ray diffraction image of DNA
	- Revealed the helical structure and key dimensions
	- Showed the sugar-phosphate backbone was on the outside
- Meticulous experimentalist who insisted on rigorous data before drawing conclusions
- Her data was shared with Watson and Crick **without her knowledge or consent**
- Died of ovarian cancer at 37; never received the Nobel Prize (awarded posthumously to no one)

### Maurice Wilkins (1916–2004)
- Also at King's College London; worked on X-ray diffraction of DNA
- Had a strained working relationship with Franklin
- Showed Franklin's Photo 51 to James Watson — a pivotal and controversial act
- Shared the 1962 Nobel Prize with Watson and Crick

### James Watson (b. 1928) & Francis Crick (1916–2004)
- Working at the Cavendish Laboratory, Cambridge
- Combined insights from multiple sources
	- Franklin's X-ray data (the helical shape and dimensions)
	- Erwin Chargaff's rules (A pairs with T; C pairs with G)
	- Linus Pauling's model-building approach
- Built physical models until the pieces fit
- Published the structure in *Nature* on April 25, 1953

## The Double Helix

- **Two complementary strands** wound around each other in a right-handed helix
- Sugar-phosphate backbone on the outside; bases on the inside
- **Base pairing rules**
	- Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) — two hydrogen bonds
	- Cytosine (C) pairs with Guanine (G) — three hydrogen bonds
- The strands are **antiparallel** — they run in opposite directions (5'→3' and 3'→5')
- The structure immediately suggests a mechanism for replication: **unzip the strands, and each serves as a template**

## Timeline

- **1944** — Avery identifies DNA as the transforming principle
- **1950** — Chargaff discovers base-pairing rules (A=T, C=G)
- **1951** — Franklin begins X-ray work on DNA at King's College
- **1952** — Franklin captures Photo 51
- **1953** — Watson and Crick publish the double helix model in *Nature*
- **1962** — Watson, Crick, and Wilkins receive the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine

## Legacy

- Launched the field of **molecular biology**
- Made possible: gene sequencing, genetic engineering, PCR, CRISPR, forensic DNA analysis
- Raised enduring questions about scientific credit, ethics, and the treatment of women in science

```osmosis
What are Chargaff's base-pairing rules?
***
In DNA, **adenine (A) always pairs with thymine (T)**, and **cytosine (C) always pairs with guanine (G)**. This means the amount of A equals T, and the amount of C equals G in any DNA molecule.
```

```osmosis

Why does the structure of the double helix immediately suggest a mechanism for replication?
***
Because the two strands are **complementary** — each strand contains all the information needed to reconstruct the other. Unzip the helix, and each strand serves as a **template** to build a new partner strand following the base-pairing rules (A↔T, C↔G).
```
