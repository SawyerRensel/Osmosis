# Review Systems

A knowledge base without review is just storage. Regular review transforms captured information into internalized understanding and actionable insight.

## Weekly Review Process

The weekly review is the most important habit for maintaining a healthy knowledge system. Based on David Allen's GTD methodology, adapted for PKM.

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Clear the inbox** (10 min)
	- Process all fleeting notes from the week
	- File, link, or delete each one
	- Goal: inbox zero by end of review
2. **Review active projects** (10 min)
	- Open each project hub note
	- Update status and next actions
	- Identify stalled projects — decide to push or pause
3. **Process new notes** (15 min)
	- Review notes created this week
	- Add missing links and tags
	- Ensure each note has at least one incoming or outgoing link
4. **Revisit recent connections** (10 min)
	- Look at backlinks on recently edited notes
	- Explore unexpected connections in graph view
	- Create new links where you see relationships
5. **Plan next week** (5 min)
	- Identify top 3 priorities
	- Schedule focused time for deep work
	- Set intentions for learning goals

### Weekly Review Checklist

- [ ] Inbox is empty
- [ ] All fleeting notes processed or deleted
- [ ] Active projects have current next actions
- [ ] New notes are linked into the knowledge graph
- [ ] Orphan notes addressed (linked or archived)
- [ ] Spaced repetition queue reviewed
- [ ] Calendar checked for upcoming deadlines
- [ ] Top 3 priorities for next week identified

## Spaced Repetition Integration

Spaced repetition is the most efficient way to move knowledge from notes into long-term memory.

### How It Works with Obsidian
- Create flashcards directly in your notes using Osmosis
- Cards are scheduled using the FSRS algorithm
- Review due cards daily (5-15 minutes)
- Difficult cards appear more frequently until mastered

### What to Make Cards For
- Core concepts you want to internalize
- Definitions and terminology in a new field
- Key relationships between ideas
- Procedures and processes you need to recall
- Facts that support your arguments or thinking

### What NOT to Make Cards For
- Information you can easily look up
- Trivial details that don't support understanding
- Content you haven't processed and understood first
- Opinions or preferences (unless studying a domain)

### Effective Card Design
- One fact or concept per card
- Use cloze deletions for definitions
- Use basic Q&A for understanding checks
- Use bidirectional cards for term-definition pairs
- Keep answers concise — if you need a paragraph, split the card

## Progressive Summarization

Tiago Forte's method for distilling notes over time through multiple review passes.

### The Five Layers

1. **Layer 0**: Original source (article, book, lecture)
2. **Layer 1**: Captured notes — the passages you saved
3. **Layer 2**: Bold the most important passages
4. **Layer 3**: Highlight the best of the bolded passages
5. **Layer 4**: Write a brief executive summary in your own words

### When to Apply Each Layer
- Layer 1: During initial capture
- Layer 2: First time you revisit the note (often during weekly review)
- Layer 3: When you need the note for a specific project
- Layer 4: When the note is central to your current thinking
- Most notes never get past Layer 2 — and that's fine

### Benefits
- Just-in-time processing (don't over-invest upfront)
- Each layer makes the note more useful for future retrieval
- Visual scanning becomes fast with bold and highlights
- The summary (Layer 4) is your own understanding, not the author's words

## Building a Review Habit

### Start Small
- Week 1-2: Just clear your inbox (5 min)
- Week 3-4: Add project review (15 min total)
- Week 5+: Full weekly review (30-50 min)

### Anchor to an Existing Habit
- Do your review every Sunday morning with coffee
- Or Friday afternoon as the last task of the work week
- Consistency matters more than duration

### Track Your Reviews
- Log each review in your daily note
- Note what you discovered or connected
- Over time, this log becomes a record of your intellectual growth
