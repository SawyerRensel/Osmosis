# Story Structure

Great stories aren't accidents — they follow patterns that have resonated with audiences for millennia. Understanding structure gives you a scaffold to build on, not a cage to write inside.

## The Three-Act Structure

The backbone of Western storytelling. Every film, novel, and campfire tale echoes this shape.

### Act I — Setup (25%)

The world before the storm. We meet the protagonist in their ordinary world, understand what they want, and watch as something disrupts everything.

- **Opening Image**: A snapshot of the protagonist's world before change
  - Sets tone, mood, and stakes
  - Often mirrored by the final image
- **Exposition**: Establish the rules
  - Who is the protagonist?
  - What do they want? (external goal)
  - What do they *need*? (internal growth)
- **Inciting Incident**: The event that changes everything
  - Must be external and undeniable
  - Forces a choice — the protagonist can't ignore it
  - Examples:
    - A letter arrives (Harry Potter)
    - A murder occurs (most mysteries)
    - A stranger rides into town (Westerns)
- **First Plot Point / Break into Two**: The protagonist commits
  - Crosses a threshold — no going back
  - Often a physical journey begins
  - The "old world" is left behind

### Act II — Confrontation (50%)

The long middle. Rising action, complications, and the protagonist being tested. This is where most writers struggle.

- **Rising Action**: Escalating obstacles
  - Each obstacle is harder than the last
  - The protagonist gains skills and allies
  - Subplots weave in and out
- **Midpoint**: A major shift
  - False victory or false defeat
  - New information changes the game
  - Stakes become personal
    - What was external becomes internal
    - The protagonist's flaw is exposed
- **Pinch Points**: Reminders of the antagonist's power
  - First pinch: ~37% mark
  - Second pinch: ~62% mark
  - Keep tension from sagging
- **All Is Lost / Dark Night of the Soul**
  - The protagonist hits rock bottom
  - Mentor dies, ally betrays, plan fails
  - Internal flaw fully exposed
  - Must choose: give up or transform

### Act III — Resolution (25%)

The final push. The protagonist, transformed by their journey, faces the ultimate test.

- **Break into Three**: A new plan emerges
  - Often inspired by a forgotten clue from Act I
  - The protagonist synthesizes everything they've learned
- **Climax**: The final confrontation
  - External goal and internal need collide
  - The protagonist must choose who they've become
  - Maximum tension — everything at stake
- **Resolution / Denouement**
  - New equilibrium established
  - Subplots resolved
  - Final image mirrors the opening — showing change

## The Hero's Journey (Monomyth)

Joseph Campbell's mythic structure, refined by Christopher Vogler for screenwriters.

### Departure

- **The Ordinary World**: Life before the adventure
- **Call to Adventure**: A challenge or quest appears
- **Refusal of the Call**: Fear, doubt, obligation
- **Meeting the Mentor**: Guidance and gifts
- **Crossing the First Threshold**: Entering the unknown

### Initiation

- **Tests, Allies, and Enemies**: Learning the new world's rules
- **Approach to the Inmost Cave**: Preparing for the ordeal
- **The Ordeal**: Death and rebirth — the central crisis
- **Reward (Seizing the Sword)**: The prize is won

### Return

- **The Road Back**: Consequences of the ordeal
- **Resurrection**: Final test, using everything learned
- **Return with the Elixir**: The hero brings wisdom home

## Story Beats (Blake Snyder's Save the Cat!)

A more granular beat sheet used widely in screenwriting.

- **Opening Image** (1%): Before snapshot
- **Theme Stated** (5%): Someone states the lesson the hero must learn
- **Setup** (1-10%): The hero's world, flaws, and stakes
- **Catalyst** (10%): The inciting incident
- **Debate** (10-20%): Should I go? Can I do this?
- **Break into Two** (20%): The hero chooses to act
- **B Story** (22%): The love interest or mentor subplot
- **Fun and Games** (20-50%): The "promise of the premise"
- **Midpoint** (50%): False victory or false defeat
- **Bad Guys Close In** (50-75%): Internal and external pressure mounts
- **All Is Lost** (75%): The lowest point
- **Dark Night of the Soul** (75-80%): Despair before dawn
- **Break into Three** (80%): Eureka! A and B stories merge
- **Finale** (80-99%): The hero storms the castle
- **Final Image** (99%): After snapshot — proof of change
