# Magic Systems

The magic system is the engine of a fantasy world. It determines what's possible, what's at stake, and how conflict resolves. A well-designed magic system makes the impossible feel inevitable.

## Hard Magic vs. Soft Magic

This isn't a binary — it's a spectrum. Most great fantasy falls somewhere in between.

### Hard Magic

Magic with clearly defined rules, costs, and limitations that the reader understands.

- **Characteristics**:
  - Explicit rules explained to the reader
  - Consistent limitations and costs
  - Characters solve problems using magical mechanics
  - Reader can predict what's possible
- **Strengths**:
  - Enables satisfying puzzle-like plots
  - Characters earn victories through cleverness
  - Creates a sense of fairness
- **Risks**:
  - Can feel mechanical or game-like
  - Over-explanation kills wonder
  - Rule-lawyering replaces storytelling
- **Examples**:
  - Allomancy (Mistborn) — metals grant specific powers
  - Bending (Avatar) — elemental manipulation with physical discipline
  - Sympathy (Kingkiller Chronicle) — energy conservation applied to magic
  - Alchemy (Fullmetal Alchemist) — equivalent exchange

### Soft Magic

Magic that is mysterious, unexplained, and operates on narrative logic rather than mechanical rules.

- **Characteristics**:
  - Rules are vague or unknown to the reader
  - Magic creates wonder and atmosphere
  - Limitations are implied, not stated
  - Serves theme and emotion over mechanics
- **Strengths**:
  - Preserves sense of wonder
  - Flexible for storytelling
  - Feels mythic and timeless
- **Risks**:
  - Can feel like deus ex machina
  - Hard to create tension if anything is possible
  - Reader may feel cheated by arbitrary outcomes
- **Examples**:
  - The Force (Star Wars, original trilogy) — mysterious, spiritual
  - Gandalf's magic (Lord of the Rings) — rarely explained, always restrained
  - The magic of Earthsea (Le Guin) — true names hold power
  - The Old Magic (Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell) — wild and unpredictable

### Comparison

| Aspect | Hard Magic | Soft Magic |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Rules | Explicit, systematic | Vague, atmospheric |
| Reader knowledge | High — understands the system | Low — magic is mysterious |
| Problem solving | Characters use magic cleverly | Magic isn't the primary tool |
| Tension source | Limitations and costs | Uncertainty and awe |
| Risk | Feels like a video game | Feels like deus ex machina |
| Best for | Plot-driven stories | Atmosphere-driven stories |
| Tone | Scientific, rational | Mythic, numinous |

## Sanderson's Laws of Magic

Brandon Sanderson codified three principles that help writers design effective magic systems.

### First Law: Problem Solving

> An author's ability to solve problems with magic in a satisfying way is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic.

- If magic solves the climax, the reader must understand the rules
- Soft magic can *create* problems but shouldn't *solve* them
- The more a character relies on magic, the harder the system needs to be
- This is about reader satisfaction, not worldbuilding thoroughness

### Second Law: Limitations

> Limitations are more interesting than powers.

- What a character *can't* do is more compelling than what they can
- Costs create drama:
  - Physical exhaustion or pain
  - Moral compromise
  - Finite resources
  - Unintended consequences
- Weaknesses force creativity
  - Superman is boring invincible; kryptonite makes him interesting
  - Allomancers running out of metal mid-fight
  - Benders needing their element present

### Third Law: Expansion

> Expand what you have before you add something new.

- Explore the implications of existing magic before adding new powers
- Deep systems are more satisfying than broad ones
- Ask: "What would society *actually* look like if this magic existed?"
  - Economic implications
  - Military applications
  - Social stratification
  - Legal systems around magic use

## Designing Your System

### Step 1: Start with Theme

What does magic *mean* in your story? Magic should reinforce your theme.

- Power and corruption: Magic that tempts and corrupts
- Knowledge and sacrifice: Magic that demands understanding
- Connection and empathy: Magic rooted in relationships
- Nature and balance: Magic tied to the natural world

### Step 2: Define the Source

- Where does magical energy come from?
- Is it finite or renewable?
- Can it be stored, transferred, or stolen?

### Step 3: Set Hard Limits

- What is absolutely impossible?
- What is the cost of using magic?
- What happens if you push too far?

### Step 4: Test Against Your Plot

- Does the magic system create or solve your central conflict?
- Are there scenes where magic makes things too easy?
- Does the system force interesting character choices?
