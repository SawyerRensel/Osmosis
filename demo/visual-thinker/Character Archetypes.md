# Character Archetypes

Carl Jung proposed that certain character patterns recur across all cultures and eras because they tap into the collective unconscious. These archetypes aren't rigid molds — they're gravitational centers that characters orbit around, bending and combining in infinite ways.

## The Hero

The central figure who drives the story forward through action and sacrifice.

- **Core Drive**: To prove worth through courageous action
- **Strengths**: Courage, determination, self-sacrifice
- **Weaknesses**: Arrogance, overconfidence, savior complex
- **Shadow Side**: The bully, the obsessive
- **Examples in Fiction**:
  - Frodo Baggins — the reluctant hero
  - Katniss Everdeen — the unwilling symbol
  - Luke Skywalker — the classic mythic hero
  - Moana — the called hero who answers despite fear

## The Shadow

The antagonist — often a dark mirror of the hero. The Shadow represents what the hero could become without growth.

- **Core Drive**: To dominate, destroy, or corrupt
- **Strengths**: Power, intelligence, ruthlessness
- **Weaknesses**: Isolation, inability to connect
- **Shadow Side**: Every hero carries a shadow within
- **Examples in Fiction**:
  - Darth Vader — the hero fallen
  - Sauron — pure embodiment of domination
  - Amy Dunne (Gone Girl) — the shadow as protagonist
  - The White Witch — cold authority without compassion

## The Mentor

The guide who prepares the hero for the journey ahead. Often represents wisdom the hero hasn't yet earned.

- **Core Drive**: To teach, protect, and pass on knowledge
- **Strengths**: Wisdom, experience, patience
- **Weaknesses**: Can become overprotective or withholding
- **Shadow Side**: The manipulator who uses the hero as a pawn
- **Examples in Fiction**:
  - Gandalf — the wandering guide
  - Dumbledore — wisdom with secrets
  - Haymitch Abernathy — the broken mentor
  - Mr. Miyagi — patience embodied

## The Trickster

The agent of chaos who disrupts the status quo. Tricksters reveal truth through misdirection and humor.

- **Core Drive**: To disrupt, amuse, and reveal hidden truths
- **Strengths**: Wit, adaptability, seeing through pretense
- **Weaknesses**: Unreliability, self-interest, cruelty
- **Shadow Side**: The con artist, the sociopath
- **Examples in Fiction**:
  - Loki — chaos as identity
  - The Cheshire Cat — unsettling wisdom
  - Fred and George Weasley — joyful subversion
  - Jack Sparrow — the trickster as protagonist

## The Herald

The character or event that announces change. The Herald sets the story in motion.

- **Core Drive**: To announce change and challenge the hero
- **Strengths**: Clarity, urgency, catalytic energy
- **Examples in Fiction**:
  - The White Rabbit — "You're late!"
  - R2-D2's holographic message
  - Hagrid arriving on Harry's birthday

## The Threshold Guardian

Tests the hero before they can advance. Not necessarily villains — they ensure the hero is ready.

- **Core Drive**: To test worthiness
- **Strengths**: Strength, loyalty to their post
- **Examples in Fiction**:
  - The Sphinx — riddles before passage
  - Cerberus — guardian of the underworld
  - The bouncer at the exclusive club

## The Shapeshifter

A character whose loyalty or nature is uncertain. Keeps the hero (and reader) guessing.

- **Core Drive**: To change, conceal, and destabilize
- **Strengths**: Adaptability, mystery, allure
- **Weaknesses**: Untrustworthiness, identity confusion
- **Examples in Fiction**:
  - Severus Snape — whose side is he on?
  - Catwoman — ally or enemy?
  - Gollum — split between Sméagol and the Ring's slave
