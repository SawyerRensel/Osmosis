# Dialogue Techniques

Dialogue does more than convey information — it reveals character, advances plot, builds tension, and creates rhythm. Every line a character speaks should do at least two of these things simultaneously.

[Writing Great Dialogue — Brandon Sanderson](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SbqSfGfWwFs)

## Subtext

What characters *mean* versus what they *say*. The best dialogue lives in the gap between the two.

- **Why Subtext Matters**:
  - Real people rarely say exactly what they mean
  - Subtext creates tension and depth
  - It rewards attentive readers
  - It makes characters feel three-dimensional
- **How to Write It**:
  - Characters talk *around* the real issue
  - Actions contradict words
  - Context charges neutral words with meaning
  - What's left unsaid carries the weight
- **Techniques for Building Subtext**:
  - **Deflection**: Answering a different question than the one asked
  - **Understatement**: Saying less than the situation warrants
  - **Overcompensation**: Talking too much to hide discomfort
  - **Loaded silence**: Pausing where a response is expected

Example — a couple after an argument:

```text
"Did you eat?" she asked.
"I'm fine."
"There's pasta in the fridge."
"I said I'm fine."
She turned back to the sink. "It'll go bad if nobody eats it."
```

Neither character mentions the argument. The pasta isn't really about pasta.

## Pacing & Rhythm

Dialogue has musicality. Short lines accelerate tension. Long speeches slow the reader down and demand attention.

- **Fast Pacing** (short, clipped exchanges):
  - Builds tension and urgency
  - Works for arguments, action, rapid decisions
  - Minimal attribution — the rhythm carries it
- **Slow Pacing** (longer speeches, description between lines):
  - Creates weight and gravity
  - Works for revelations, emotional beats, philosophy
  - Use sparingly — long speeches risk losing the reader
- **Rhythm Variation**:
  - Alternate between fast and slow within a scene
  - A sudden short line after a long speech creates impact
  - Silence (a beat, a pause) is a rhythmic tool

Example — fast pacing in conflict:

```text
"You told her."
"She already knew."
"She didn't. I made sure—"
"You made sure of nothing, David."
```

Example — slow pacing for revelation:

```text
The old woman set down her teacup with a deliberate click against
the saucer. "Your mother," she said, "was not the person you
think she was. None of us were, back then. The war changed
everyone, and your mother — she changed more than most."
```

## Voice & Individuality

Every character should sound distinct. A reader should be able to identify the speaker without attribution tags.

- **Elements of Voice**:
  - Vocabulary level and word choice
  - Sentence length and structure
  - Rhythm and cadence
  - Verbal tics and catchphrases (use sparingly)
  - What topics they gravitate toward
  - What they avoid saying
- **Building Distinct Voices**:
  - Education level affects vocabulary
  - Profession shapes metaphors
    - A sailor speaks in nautical terms
    - A doctor clinical, precise
    - A poet circuitous, lyrical
  - Emotional state affects sentence structure
    - Anxious characters: fragmented, interrupted
    - Confident characters: declarative, complete
    - Grieving characters: sparse, trailing off
  - Cultural background shapes idiom and expression

## Dialect & Speech Patterns

Representing how characters sound without making dialogue unreadable.

- **Do**:
  - Use word choice and syntax to suggest dialect
  - Sprinkle in occasional phonetic spelling for flavor
  - Let rhythm do the heavy lifting
  - Research real speech patterns respectfully
- **Don't**:
  - Write entire passages in phonetic spelling (exhausting to read)
  - Use dialect only for marginalized characters (problematic)
  - Rely on stereotypes
  - Make dialect a punchline

Example — suggesting dialect through word choice:

```text
"I reckon we ought to head back before the light goes. Ain't
nothing good comes of wandering these hills after dark."
```

Not:

```text
"Ah reck'n we oughta head bahk b'fore th' laht goes..."
```

## Attribution & Beats

How you tag dialogue and break it up with action.

- **"Said" Is Invisible**:
  - Readers' eyes skip over "said" — it doesn't interrupt flow
  - Alternatives ("exclaimed," "declared," "opined") draw attention to themselves
  - Use alternatives only when "said" genuinely doesn't work
- **Action Beats Replace Tags**:
  - Instead of "she said angrily," show the anger
  - Action beats ground dialogue in physical space
  - They reveal character through behavior
- **When to Use No Tag**:
  - Two-person scenes with established rhythm
  - Fast-paced exchanges where tags would slow things down
  - When voice is distinct enough to identify the speaker

Example — action beats vs. attribution:

```text
Instead of:
"I can't believe you did that," she said angrily.

Write:
She slammed the folder on the desk. "I can't believe you did that."
```

## Exposition Through Dialogue

Delivering necessary information without it feeling like a lecture.

- **The "As You Know, Bob" Problem**:
  - Characters explaining things they both already know
  - Feels artificial and breaks immersion
  - The single most common dialogue mistake in genre fiction
- **Better Approaches**:
  - Have a character who genuinely doesn't know (the newcomer, the student)
  - Reveal information through conflict — characters argue about what it *means*, not what it *is*
  - Spread exposition across multiple scenes
  - Use subtext — what characters *don't* explain tells us they take it for granted
