# Plot Devices

Plot devices are storytelling tools — mechanisms that move the narrative forward, create tension, or deliver meaning. They're neither good nor bad; what matters is how skillfully they're used.

## Chekhov's Gun

> "If in the first act you have hung a pistol on the wall, then in the following one it should be fired."
> — Anton Chekhov

Every element in a story should be necessary. If you introduce something, it must pay off.

- **How to Use It Well**:
  - Plant details early that become crucial later
  - Make the setup feel natural, not conspicuous
  - The payoff should feel inevitable in hindsight
- **Common Mistakes**:
  - Introducing elements that never pay off (broken promise to the reader)
  - Making the setup too obvious (telegraphing the twist)
  - Forgetting about planted elements (continuity errors)
- **Examples**:
  - The Marauder's Map appearing before it becomes essential (Harry Potter)
  - The conch shell in Lord of the Flies — symbol of order that shatters
  - Every item in a Pixar movie's first ten minutes

## Red Herring

A clue or element designed to mislead the audience, diverting attention from the truth.

- **How to Use It Well**:
  - Must be plausible enough to genuinely mislead
  - Should be "fair" — the reader shouldn't feel cheated
  - Resolution should make the misdirection clear
- **Common Mistakes**:
  - Too obvious — reader sees through it immediately
  - Too obscure — reader feels tricked, not surprised
  - Leaving red herrings unresolved
- **Examples**:
  - Every suspect in an Agatha Christie novel
  - Snape's antagonism hiding his true loyalty (Harry Potter)
  - The "obvious suspect" in any good mystery

## MacGuffin

An object or goal that drives the plot but has little intrinsic importance. The characters care about it; what it *is* doesn't really matter.

- **How to Use It Well**:
  - Keep the focus on character conflict, not the object
  - The MacGuffin should motivate action, not explain it
  - It can be vague — mystery adds to its power
- **Common Mistakes**:
  - Over-explaining the MacGuffin (it should stay simple)
  - Making the MacGuffin more interesting than the characters
  - Forgetting that the audience needs to care *why* characters want it
- **Examples**:
  - The briefcase in Pulp Fiction — we never learn what's inside
  - The Maltese Falcon — everyone wants it, it's a fake
  - The One Ring — arguably transcends MacGuffin status because of its thematic weight
  - The Infinity Stones — cosmic MacGuffins that drive an entire franchise

## Deus Ex Machina

A sudden, unexpected solution to an impossible problem — literally "god from the machine."

- **When It Works**:
  - Comedy and satire (used deliberately for humor)
  - When the *point* is that life is absurd
  - Ancient Greek theater (where it was literal — a god descends)
- **Why It Usually Fails**:
  - Robs the protagonist of agency
  - Breaks the reader's trust
  - Makes earlier struggles feel pointless
  - Violates Sanderson's First Law
- **How to Avoid It**:
  - Foreshadow the solution
  - Ensure the protagonist's actions contribute to the resolution
  - Establish rules early that make the solution possible
- **Examples**:
  - The Eagles in Lord of the Rings (debated endlessly)
  - A previously unmentioned character arriving to save the day
  - A sudden inheritance solving all financial problems

## Dramatic Irony

The audience knows something the characters don't. One of the most powerful tools for generating tension.

- **How to Use It Well**:
  - Create scenes where the audience wants to scream warnings at characters
  - Use it to add layers of meaning to dialogue
  - Build tension through the gap between what we know and what characters know
- **Types of Dramatic Irony**:
  - **Tragic irony**: The audience knows the hero is doomed
    - Oedipus searching for the murderer *he* is
    - Romeo believing Juliet is dead
  - **Suspense irony**: The audience sees danger the character doesn't
    - The bomb under the table (Hitchcock's famous example)
    - A character trusting someone we know is a traitor
  - **Comic irony**: The mismatch creates humor
    - Mistaken identity in Shakespeare's comedies
    - Characters unknowingly describing themselves
- **Examples**:
  - The audience knowing Darth Vader is Luke's father before Luke does
  - Shakespeare's entire catalog
  - Horror films where we see the killer but the character doesn't

## Unreliable Narrator

The storyteller's account cannot be fully trusted, forcing the reader to question everything.

- **Types**:
  - **Deliberately deceptive**: The narrator lies to the reader
  - **Self-deluded**: The narrator genuinely believes their distorted version
  - **Naive**: The narrator lacks understanding (often a child)
  - **Mentally unstable**: Perception is distorted
- **How to Use It Well**:
  - Plant subtle inconsistencies the attentive reader can catch
  - The reveal should recontextualize everything that came before
  - Give the reader enough to suspect, not enough to be certain
- **Examples**:
  - Gone Girl — both narrators lie
  - Fight Club — the narrator's reality fractures
  - The Remains of the Day — Stevens can't admit his own feelings
  - We Have Always Lived in the Castle — Merricat's perspective distorts truth
