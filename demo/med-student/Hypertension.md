# Hypertension

Hypertension is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and chronic kidney disease. It affects nearly half of all adults in the United States and is often called the "silent killer" because it is usually asymptomatic until end-organ damage occurs.

## Blood Pressure Classification (ACC/AHA 2017)

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Normal** | < 120 | and | < 80 |
| **Elevated** | 120–129 | and | < 80 |
| **Stage 1 HTN** | 130–139 | or | 80–89 |
| **Stage 2 HTN** | ≥ 140 | or | ≥ 90 |
| **Hypertensive crisis** | > 180 | and/or | > 120 |

## Etiology

- **Primary (essential) hypertension** — 90-95% of cases
    - Multifactorial: genetics, diet (sodium), obesity, sedentary lifestyle, stress
    - No identifiable single cause
    - Onset typically age 25-55
- **Secondary hypertension** — 5-10% of cases
    - Renal artery stenosis (fibromuscular dysplasia in young women, atherosclerosis in older adults)
    - Primary aldosteronism (Conn syndrome) — most common endocrine cause
    - Pheochromocytoma (episodic HTN, headache, sweating, palpitations)
    - Cushing syndrome
    - Coarctation of the aorta (upper extremity HTN, rib notching on CXR)
    - Obstructive sleep apnea
    - Thyroid disease (hyper- or hypothyroidism)

## End-Organ Damage

- **Heart**: Left ventricular hypertrophy → heart failure, coronary artery disease
- **Brain**: Stroke (ischemic and hemorrhagic), vascular dementia
- **Kidneys**: Hypertensive nephrosclerosis → chronic kidney disease
- **Eyes**: Hypertensive retinopathy (AV nicking, cotton-wool spots, papilledema)
- **Vasculature**: Aortic dissection, peripheral artery disease, aneurysm

## Treatment Algorithm

1. **Lifestyle modifications** (all patients, all stages)
    1. DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
    2. Sodium restriction (< 2,300 mg/day, ideally < 1,500 mg/day)
    3. Regular aerobic exercise (150 min/week)
    4. Weight loss (target BMI < 25)
    5. Limit alcohol (≤ 2 drinks/day men, ≤ 1 drink/day women)
    6. Smoking cessation
2. **Elevated BP (120-129/<80)**: Lifestyle modifications alone
3. **Stage 1 (130-139/80-89)**
    1. If 10-year ASCVD risk < 10%: lifestyle modifications, reassess in 3-6 months
    2. If 10-year ASCVD risk ≥ 10% or known CVD/DM/CKD: initiate one antihypertensive
4. **Stage 2 (≥140/≥90)**: Initiate two antihypertensives from different classes + lifestyle
5. **Hypertensive crisis (>180/>120)**
    1. Hypertensive urgency (no end-organ damage): oral medications, gradual reduction
    2. Hypertensive emergency (with end-organ damage): IV medications in ICU, reduce MAP by ≤ 25% in first hour

## First-Line Antihypertensives

- **Thiazide diuretics** (chlorthalidone, HCTZ) — preferred in uncomplicated HTN
- **ACE inhibitors / ARBs** — preferred in DM, CKD with proteinuria, HF
- **Calcium channel blockers** (amlodipine) — preferred in elderly, Black patients
- **Combination therapy**: ACEi + CCB or ACEi + thiazide (do NOT combine ACEi + ARB)

What is the most common cause of secondary hypertension from an endocrine source?
***
**Primary aldosteronism (Conn syndrome)** is the most common endocrine cause of secondary hypertension. It is characterized by excess aldosterone production (usually from an adrenal adenoma), causing sodium retention, potassium wasting, and hypertension. Screen with aldosterone-to-renin ratio.
