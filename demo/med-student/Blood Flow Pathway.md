# Blood Flow Pathway

Understanding the complete pathway of blood flow through the heart and vascular system is fundamental to cardiology. Blood follows a predictable, unidirectional path maintained by the heart's valves.

![Blood circulation diagram](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg/800px-2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg)

## Complete Cardiac Cycle — Blood Flow Sequence

1. Deoxygenated blood returns to the **right atrium**
    1. Via the superior vena cava (head, neck, upper limbs)
    2. Via the inferior vena cava (trunk, lower limbs)
    3. Via the coronary sinus (cardiac veins)
2. Blood passes through the **tricuspid valve**
3. Blood enters the **right ventricle**
4. Right ventricle contracts → blood passes through the **pulmonary valve**
5. Blood enters the **pulmonary trunk**, which bifurcates into:
    1. Right pulmonary artery → right lung
    2. Left pulmonary artery → left lung
6. Gas exchange occurs in **pulmonary capillaries**
    - CO₂ diffuses out into alveoli
    - O₂ diffuses into blood, binding hemoglobin
7. Oxygenated blood returns via **four pulmonary veins**
    1. Two right pulmonary veins
    2. Two left pulmonary veins
8. Blood enters the **left atrium**
9. Blood passes through the **mitral (bicuspid) valve**
10. Blood enters the **left ventricle**
11. Left ventricle contracts → blood passes through the **aortic valve**
12. Blood enters the **aorta** and is distributed systemically

## Pulmonary Circulation

The low-pressure circuit between the heart and lungs.

- **Purpose**: Gas exchange — load O₂, unload CO₂
- **Pressure**: Mean pulmonary artery pressure ~15 mmHg
- **Key feature**: Pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood (unique among arteries)
- **Key feature**: Pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood (unique among veins)

## Systemic Circulation

The high-pressure circuit supplying the entire body.

- **Purpose**: Deliver O₂ and nutrients, remove CO₂ and waste
- **Pressure**: Mean arterial pressure ~93 mmHg
- **Key feature**: Aorta branches into progressively smaller arteries
- **Key feature**: Venous return aided by skeletal muscle pump and venous valves

## Comparison: Pulmonary vs Systemic Circulation

| Feature | Pulmonary Circulation | Systemic Circulation |
|---|---|---|
| **Pump** | Right ventricle | Left ventricle |
| **Outflow vessel** | Pulmonary trunk | Aorta |
| **Mean pressure** | ~15 mmHg | ~93 mmHg |
| **Resistance** | Low (pulmonary vascular resistance) | High (systemic vascular resistance) |
| **Gas exchange site** | Pulmonary capillaries (alveoli) | Systemic capillaries (tissues) |
| **Arteries carry** | Deoxygenated blood | Oxygenated blood |
| **Veins carry** | Oxygenated blood | Deoxygenated blood |
| **Return vessel** | Pulmonary veins (×4) | Superior & inferior vena cava |
| **Wall thickness** | RV wall ~3-5 mm | LV wall ~13-15 mm |

## Fetal Circulation (High-Yield)

- **Ductus arteriosus**: Shunts blood from pulmonary trunk to aorta (bypasses lungs)
- **Foramen ovale**: Shunts blood from right atrium to left atrium
- **Ductus venosus**: Shunts blood from umbilical vein past the liver
- At birth, increased O₂ and decreased prostaglandins trigger closure of these shunts

What is unique about the pulmonary arteries and pulmonary veins compared to their systemic counterparts?
***
**Pulmonary arteries** carry **deoxygenated** blood (opposite of systemic arteries), and **pulmonary veins** carry **oxygenated** blood (opposite of systemic veins). This is because the naming convention is based on direction of flow relative to the heart, not oxygen content.
