# Valvular Disease

Valvular heart disease involves damage to or a defect in one of the four heart valves. Pathology is classified as stenosis (failure to open fully) or regurgitation (failure to close fully). Severity ranges from mild (asymptomatic) to severe (requiring surgical intervention).

## Aortic Valve

- **Aortic Stenosis (AS)**
    - Most common valvular disease requiring surgery in developed countries
    - Causes
        - Degenerative calcification (age > 65, most common)
        - Bicuspid aortic valve (most common cause in patients < 65)
        - Rheumatic heart disease
    - Pathophysiology: LV pressure overload → concentric hypertrophy → eventual failure
    - Classic triad of symptoms: **Syncope, Angina, Dyspnea** (SAD)
    - Murmur: Crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur at right upper sternal border (RUSB)
        - Radiates to carotids
        - Pulsus parvus et tardus (weak, delayed carotid upstroke)
        - Peaks later in systole with increasing severity
    - Management: Surgical aortic valve replacement (SAVR) or transcatheter (TAVR) when symptomatic
- **Aortic Regurgitation (AR)**
    - Causes
        - Acute: infective endocarditis, aortic dissection, trauma
        - Chronic: bicuspid aortic valve, Marfan syndrome, rheumatic disease, syphilitic aortitis
    - Pathophysiology: Volume overload → eccentric hypertrophy → LV dilation
    - Murmur: Early diastolic decrescendo "blowing" murmur at left sternal border
        - Best heard sitting up, leaning forward, end-expiration
        - Austin Flint murmur (low-pitched diastolic rumble from regurgitant jet hitting mitral leaflet)
    - Classic signs: Wide pulse pressure, bounding "water-hammer" pulses, head bobbing (de Musset sign)

## Mitral Valve

- **Mitral Stenosis (MS)**
    - Causes
        - Rheumatic heart disease (most common cause worldwide by far)
        - Calcification in elderly
    - Pathophysiology: Impaired LV filling → LA dilation → pulmonary congestion
    - Murmur: Low-pitched diastolic rumble at apex, best heard in left lateral decubitus
        - Opening snap followed by rumble
        - Louder with exercise, shorter S2–OS interval = more severe
    - Complications: Atrial fibrillation (from LA dilation), thromboembolism, pulmonary hypertension
- **Mitral Regurgitation (MR)**
    - Most common valvular disorder overall
    - Causes
        - Acute: papillary muscle rupture (post-MI), chordae tendineae rupture, infective endocarditis
        - Chronic: mitral valve prolapse (most common in developed countries), rheumatic, dilated cardiomyopathy
    - Murmur: Holosystolic (pansystolic) "blowing" murmur at apex
        - Radiates to left axilla
        - Louder with increased afterload (hand grip maneuver)
    - Mitral valve prolapse: Mid-systolic click followed by late systolic murmur

## Tricuspid Valve

- **Tricuspid Regurgitation (TR)**
    - Usually functional (secondary to RV dilation from pulmonary hypertension or left heart failure)
    - Primary causes: infective endocarditis (IV drug users), carcinoid syndrome, Ebstein anomaly
    - Murmur: Holosystolic at left lower sternal border
    - Augmented by inspiration (Carvallo sign)
    - Jugular venous distension with prominent V waves

## Pulmonary Valve

- **Pulmonary Stenosis**
    - Usually congenital
    - Murmur: Systolic ejection murmur at left upper sternal border
    - Increases with inspiration

| Aortic stenosis | Systolic | RUSB | Crescendo-decrescendo | Radiates to carotids |
