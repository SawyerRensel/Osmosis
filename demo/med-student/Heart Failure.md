# Heart Failure

Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome in which the heart is unable to pump sufficient blood to meet the metabolic demands of the body, or can only do so at elevated filling pressures. It affects approximately 6 million adults in the United States.

## Pathophysiology

- **Reduced cardiac output** → activation of compensatory mechanisms
    - Sympathetic nervous system activation
        - Increased heart rate and contractility (short-term benefit)
        - Vasoconstriction → increased afterload (long-term harm)
        - β-receptor downregulation over time
    - Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)
        - Angiotensin II → vasoconstriction + aldosterone release
        - Aldosterone → sodium and water retention → volume overload
        - Cardiac remodeling → fibrosis and hypertrophy
    - Natriuretic peptides (ANP, BNP)
        - Released in response to atrial/ventricular stretch
        - Promote vasodilation and natriuresis (counterregulatory)
        - BNP and NT-proBNP used as diagnostic biomarkers
- **Frank-Starling mechanism** — initially increases stroke volume with increased preload
- **Ventricular remodeling** — progressive dilation and wall thinning → further dysfunction

## Classification by Ejection Fraction

| Type | EF | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **HFrEF** (systolic HF) | ≤ 40% | Impaired contractility; dilated ventricle; most evidence-based therapies target this |
| **HFmrEF** (mid-range) | 41–49% | Intermediate phenotype; emerging evidence for treatment |
| **HFpEF** (diastolic HF) | ≥ 50% | Impaired relaxation/filling; stiff ventricle; common in elderly, hypertensive, obese patients |

## NYHA Functional Classification

- **Class I** — No limitation of physical activity
    - Ordinary activity does not cause symptoms
    - Asymptomatic heart failure
- **Class II** — Slight limitation of physical activity
    - Comfortable at rest
    - Ordinary activity causes fatigue, dyspnea, or palpitations
- **Class III** — Marked limitation of physical activity
    - Comfortable at rest
    - Less than ordinary activity causes symptoms
- **Class IV** — Unable to carry out any physical activity without discomfort
    - Symptoms at rest
    - Any physical activity increases discomfort

## Systolic vs Diastolic Heart Failure

| Feature | Systolic (HFrEF) | Diastolic (HFpEF) |
|---|---|---|
| **Ejection fraction** | ≤ 40% | ≥ 50% |
| **Ventricle shape** | Dilated, thin-walled | Hypertrophied, stiff |
| **Primary defect** | Impaired contraction | Impaired relaxation/filling |
| **Common causes** | MI, dilated cardiomyopathy | Hypertension, aging, diabetes |
| **S3 gallop** | Common | Uncommon |
| **S4 gallop** | Uncommon | Common |
| **BNP levels** | Markedly elevated | Mildly to moderately elevated |
| **Treatment** | Strong evidence base (GDMT) | Limited evidence; treat underlying cause |

## Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy (HFrEF)

The "four pillars" of HFrEF treatment:

1. **ACE inhibitor / ARB / ARNI** (sacubitril/valsartan)
2. **Beta-blocker** (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, or bisoprolol)
3. **Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist** (spironolactone or eplerenone)
4. **SGLT2 inhibitor** (dapagliflozin or empagliflozin)

Additional therapies: loop diuretics for congestion, hydralazine/isosorbide dinitrate (especially in Black patients), ICD for EF ≤ 35%, CRT for wide QRS.
