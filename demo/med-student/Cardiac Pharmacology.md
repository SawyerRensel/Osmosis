# Cardiac Pharmacology

Cardiovascular drugs represent one of the most prescribed drug classes worldwide. Understanding their mechanisms, indications, and adverse effects is essential for clinical practice.

[Cardiac Pharmacology Overview — Ninja Nerd](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7ynlFfKriqc)

## Beta-Blockers (-olol)

- **Mechanism**: Block β-adrenergic receptors → decrease heart rate, contractility, and conduction velocity
- **Selective (β₁)**
    - Metoprolol
    - Atenolol
    - Bisoprolol
    - Esmolol (ultra-short acting, IV only)
- **Non-selective (β₁ + β₂)**
    - Propranolol (also crosses BBB → anxiolytic effects)
    - Nadolol
    - Timolol (topical for glaucoma)
- **Combined α + β blockade**
    - Carvedilol (used in heart failure)
    - Labetalol (used in hypertensive emergencies, safe in pregnancy)
- **Indications**: Hypertension, angina, heart failure (specific agents), arrhythmias, post-MI
- **Side effects**: Bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue, bronchospasm (non-selective), masking hypoglycemia in diabetics
- **Contraindications**: Decompensated heart failure, severe bradycardia, second/third-degree heart block

## ACE Inhibitors (-pril)

- **Mechanism**: Inhibit angiotensin-converting enzyme → decrease angiotensin II production → decrease aldosterone → vasodilation + reduced fluid retention
- **Common agents**
    - Lisinopril (once daily, no hepatic metabolism)
    - Enalapril (prodrug → enalaprilat)
    - Ramipril
    - Captopril (shortest acting, TID dosing)
    - Benazepril
- **Indications**: Hypertension, heart failure, post-MI, diabetic nephropathy, CKD with proteinuria
- **Side effects**: Dry cough (due to bradykinin accumulation), hyperkalemia, angioedema (rare but serious), hypotension (first-dose)
- **Contraindications**: Pregnancy (teratogenic), bilateral renal artery stenosis, history of angioedema

## Calcium Channel Blockers

- **Mechanism**: Block L-type calcium channels → decrease intracellular calcium
- **Dihydropyridines (vascular selective)**
    - Amlodipine (long-acting, once daily)
    - Nifedipine (avoid short-acting sublingual — reflex tachycardia)
    - Felodipine
    - Primarily cause vasodilation → decrease afterload
    - Reflex tachycardia may occur
- **Non-dihydropyridines (cardiac selective)**
    - Verapamil (strongest negative inotrope)
    - Diltiazem (moderate effects on both heart and vessels)
    - Decrease heart rate and contractility
    - Used for rate control in atrial fibrillation
- **Side effects**: Peripheral edema (dihydropyridines), constipation (verapamil), bradycardia (non-dihydropyridines)

## Diuretics

- **Thiazide diuretics**
    - Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), chlorthalidone
    - Act on distal convoluted tubule (DCT)
    - Inhibit Na⁺/Cl⁻ cotransporter
    - First-line for uncomplicated hypertension
    - Side effects: Hypokalemia, hyperuricemia (gout), hypercalcemia, hyperglycemia
- **Loop diuretics**
    - Furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide
    - Act on thick ascending limb of loop of Henle
    - Inhibit Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ cotransporter (NKCC2)
    - Most potent diuretics — used in acute decompensated heart failure
    - Side effects: Hypokalemia, ototoxicity, hypocalcemia, metabolic alkalosis
- **Potassium-sparing diuretics**
    - Spironolactone / eplerenone (aldosterone antagonists)
    - Amiloride / triamterene (ENaC blockers)
    - Act on collecting duct
    - Side effects: Hyperkalemia, gynecomastia (spironolactone)
    - Spironolactone reduces mortality in heart failure (RALES trial)

## Drug Comparison Table

| Drug Class | Prototype | Primary Mechanism | Key Side Effect | First-Line For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Beta-blocker | Metoprolol | β₁ receptor blockade | Bradycardia | Post-MI, HF, HTN |
| ACE inhibitor | Lisinopril | ↓ Angiotensin II | Dry cough | HTN, HF, DM nephropathy |
| CCB (DHP) | Amlodipine | Block L-type Ca²⁺ channels | Pedal edema | HTN (esp. elderly, Black patients) |
| CCB (non-DHP) | Verapamil | Block L-type Ca²⁺ channels | Constipation | Rate control in AFib |
| Thiazide | HCTZ | ↓ Na⁺/Cl⁻ reabsorption | Hypokalemia | Uncomplicated HTN |
| Loop | Furosemide | ↓ Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ reabsorption | Ototoxicity | Acute decompensated HF |
| K⁺-sparing | Spironolactone | Aldosterone antagonism | Hyperkalemia | HF (mortality benefit) |

What distinguishes dihydropyridine CCBs (e.g., amlodipine) from non-dihydropyridine CCBs (e.g., verapamil)?
***
**Dihydropyridines** are vascular selective — primarily cause vasodilation and may cause reflex tachycardia. **Non-dihydropyridines** are cardiac selective — decrease heart rate and contractility, useful for rate control in atrial fibrillation. Both block L-type calcium channels but at different tissue sites.
